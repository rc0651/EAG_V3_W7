# SEBI Circular on Algorithmic Trading and Internet-Based Trading (IBT) — Controls and Safeguards

**Circular Reference:** SEBI/HO/MRD/DOP/P/CIR/2021/577 and SEBI/HO/MRD/DOP/P/CIR/2022/0083
**Date:** July 2021 (original); May 2022 (updated provisions)
**Issued By:** Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI)
**Applicable To:** All Stock Exchanges, Stock Brokers operating Algorithmic Trading Systems and Internet-Based Trading Platforms

---

## 1. Background

Algorithmic trading (also called "algo trading") refers to the use of computer programs and automated execution systems to place orders on trading platforms without direct human intervention at the time of each order placement. Internet-Based Trading (IBT) refers to any system that allows clients to place orders through the internet directly to exchanges, including web platforms, mobile applications, and APIs.

SEBI has consistently regulated these areas to ensure that automated and internet-based trading systems do not create systemic risks, are not used for market manipulation, and that adequate human oversight is maintained over automated order generation and execution.

---

## 2. Algorithmic Trading — Safeguards Against Uncontrolled Order Flow

### 2.1 Registration and Approval of Algorithms

Every stock broker who offers algorithmic trading capabilities must:

- Obtain prior approval from the relevant stock exchange for each algorithm before deployment.
- Submit technical documentation including the algorithm's logic, maximum order rate, maximum order value per second, and any position limit checks built into the algorithm.
- Conduct system-level testing including stress tests and disaster recovery tests before deployment.
- Re-obtain exchange approval if the algorithm is modified in any manner that changes its trading logic, order rate, or risk parameters.

No algorithm may be deployed without valid exchange approval. The broker is fully responsible for all orders generated by approved algorithms even if the algorithm is provided by a third party or client.

### 2.2 Kill Switch Requirement — Halting Unconstrained Automated Order Flow

The most critical safeguard against uncontrolled algorithmic order floods is the mandatory "kill switch" mechanism. Every broker offering algo trading must implement:

**Hardware-Level Kill Switch:** A physical or electronic switch that immediately halts all order generation and cancels all pending orders from all algorithms, without requiring any software intervention. This switch must be accessible to designated risk management personnel at all times during market hours.

**Software-Level Kill Switch:** An automated system that triggers in the following circumstances to stop algorithmic order flow:
- When aggregate order value from all algorithms exceeds a pre-configured threshold in a short time window (e.g., orders exceeding Rs. 50 crores in any 5-second window)
- When the algorithm generates more than the pre-configured maximum orders per second (order rate limit breach)
- When open positions from algorithmic trading exceed pre-set position limits
- When the trading system detects an abnormal order pattern (e.g., orders placed and cancelled at an unusually high rate suggesting potential spoofing)

The kill switch must be tested at least once every quarter, and test results must be documented and available for SEBI inspection.

### 2.3 Real-Time Monitoring of Algorithmic Order Flow

Brokers must maintain a dedicated risk management desk with real-time visibility of:

- All orders generated by each algorithm (order book with algorithm ID tagging)
- Order-to-trade ratio for each algorithm (SEBI-prescribed maximum is 500 orders per executed trade for derivatives)
- Intraday position accumulation by algorithm
- P&L attribution by algorithm in real time

If the order-to-trade ratio for any algorithm exceeds the prescribed limit, the exchange may automatically disable the algorithm without prior notice. Excessive order-to-trade ratios are penalised at Rs. 1 lakh per incident on first occurrence, escalating to disabling the broker's algorithmic trading access for 5 trading days on third and subsequent occurrences.

### 2.4 Co-Location and Proximity Hosting Controls

Brokers using co-location facilities (where servers are physically located within or adjacent to exchange data centres for reduced latency) must additionally comply with:

- Audit trail requirements: All orders from co-located servers must be time-stamped to microsecond precision.
- The co-located system must have a dedicated backup system with failover capability.
- Any co-located algorithm must have identical kill switch and position limit controls as non-co-located algorithms.

### 2.5 Third-Party Algorithm Providers

When a broker deploys an algorithm developed by a third-party vendor (such as a fintech company or client-provided strategy), the broker remains fully responsible for the algorithm's compliance with all SEBI requirements. The broker must:

- Conduct due diligence on the third-party algorithm provider.
- Obtain source code review or algorithmic audit before deployment.
- Ensure the third-party algorithm is approved by the exchange in the broker's name.
- Include indemnification and liability clauses in agreements with third-party providers.

Brokers are not permitted to allow clients to directly connect their own systems to exchange trading platforms via the broker's API infrastructure without the broker exercising full oversight over the resulting order flow. Such API-based access must be treated as algorithmic trading and all safeguards must apply.

## 3. Internet-Based Trading (IBT) — Safeguards and Client Controls

### 3.1 Two-Factor Authentication

All internet-based trading platforms must implement Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) for client login. Acceptable second factors include:

- One-Time Password (OTP) sent to the client's registered mobile number
- Biometric authentication (fingerprint or facial recognition) on mobile apps
- Hardware security tokens (for institutional clients)
- Software-based authenticator app (TOTP — Time-based One-Time Password)

Username and password alone are not sufficient for trading access. Session tokens must expire after 30 minutes of inactivity.

### 3.2 Order Confirmation and Prevention of Erroneous Orders

IBT systems must implement the following controls to prevent erroneous or fraudulent orders:

- **Price Band Check:** Orders outside a specified price band relative to the last traded price (e.g., ±5% for market orders) must generate a confirmation prompt before submission.
- **Quantity Check:** Orders exceeding a pre-configured value (set by the client or broker, whichever is lower) must generate a confirmation prompt.
- **Self-Trade Prevention:** The trading system must alert clients when a new order would match against an existing order from the same client in the same security.
- **Duplicate Order Check:** Duplicate order submissions within a short time window (same security, quantity, and price within 5 seconds) must generate a confirmation prompt or be automatically rejected.

### 3.3 Client-Level Order Rate Limits for IBT

Even for non-algorithmic internet-based trading by retail clients, brokers must impose reasonable order rate limits to prevent system overload and potential market manipulation:

- Maximum order rate for retail clients: 10 orders per second per exchange
- Maximum open order value: 10 times the client's available margin
- Clients who consistently approach these limits should be flagged for review by the broker's compliance team

### 3.4 API-Based Trading Controls

Brokers offering API access to clients (whether for retail or institutional clients) must:

- Register all API users as algorithmic traders with the relevant exchange.
- Issue unique API keys per client with rate limits enforced at the API gateway level.
- Log all API-originated orders separately in audit trails.
- Conduct annual system audits of API infrastructure.
- Immediately revoke API access if suspicious trading patterns are detected.

## 4. System Audit Requirements

### 4.1 Annual System Audit

All stock brokers engaged in algorithmic trading or internet-based trading must conduct an annual system audit by a SEBI-empanelled System Auditor. The audit covers:

- Security of trading systems and data
- Adequacy of risk controls including kill switches and position limits
- Business continuity and disaster recovery plans
- Order routing and execution quality
- Compliance with data retention requirements (6 years minimum for order and trade logs)
- Adequacy of cybersecurity measures

The system audit report must be submitted to the exchange within 30 days of completion. Deficiencies noted in the audit must be remediated within the timelines specified in the audit report.

### 4.2 Incident Reporting

Brokers must report any system malfunction, outage, or anomalous trading event to the exchange and SEBI within:

- 1 hour for events that cause trading suspension or significant client impact
- 24 hours for all other system incidents affecting trading operations

Each incident report must describe the nature of the malfunction, affected clients and volumes, remedial action taken, and steps to prevent recurrence.

## 5. Governance and Oversight

### 5.1 Designated Technology Officer

Each broker engaged in algorithmic or internet-based trading must designate a senior employee as the Chief Technology Officer or Designated Technology Officer responsible for:

- Overseeing all algorithmic trading systems and internet-based trading platforms
- Approving the deployment of new algorithms and major platform changes
- Signing system audit reports
- Reporting to the board on technology risks quarterly

### 5.2 Board Oversight

The board of directors or equivalent governing body of the stock broker must:

- Approve the technology risk policy, including algorithmic trading risk parameters, at least annually
- Review system audit findings and remediation progress quarterly
- Ensure adequate budgetary provisions for technology risk management

---

## 6. Conclusion

SEBI's regulatory framework for algorithmic and internet-based trading reflects the dual imperative of enabling technological innovation in financial markets while maintaining robust safeguards against systemic risks. The kill switch mechanism, order rate limits, and real-time monitoring requirements collectively form a comprehensive defence against uncontrolled automated order flow, protecting individual investors, market integrity, and the stability of market infrastructure. Brokers who invest in robust risk management technology not only achieve compliance but also build more resilient and trusted trading platforms.
