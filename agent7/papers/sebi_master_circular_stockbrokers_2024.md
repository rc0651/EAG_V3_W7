# SEBI Master Circular for Stock Brokers — 2024

**Circular Reference:** SEBI/HO/MIRSD/MIRSD-PoD-1/P/CIR/2024/37
**Date:** May 16, 2024
**Issued By:** Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI)
**Applicable To:** All Stock Brokers, Stock Exchanges, Clearing Corporations

---

## 1. Background and Purpose

This Master Circular consolidates all earlier circulars issued by SEBI relating to stock brokers registered with SEBI under the Securities and Exchange Board of India (Stock Brokers) Regulations, 1992. The purpose is to provide a single reference document for compliance requirements, thereby reducing the compliance burden for market intermediaries while maintaining investor protection standards. All earlier standalone circulars that are subsumed herein stand superseded to the extent covered in this Master Circular.

---

## 2. Registration and Eligibility Requirements

### 2.1 Fit and Proper Criteria

Every stock broker seeking registration or renewal of registration with SEBI must satisfy the fit and proper criteria as specified in Schedule II of the SEBI (Intermediaries) Regulations, 2008. This includes:

- The applicant, its directors, partners, or principal officers must not have been convicted of any offence involving moral turpitude, fraud, or dishonesty.
- The applicant must not have been declared insolvent or bankrupt in the preceding five years.
- The applicant must maintain adequate net worth as prescribed by SEBI from time to time.
- The applicant must demonstrate financial soundness and sound track record in the securities market.

### 2.2 Net Worth Requirements

Stock brokers must maintain a minimum net worth as follows:

- Trading members in the cash segment: Rs. 50 lakhs
- Trading members in the derivatives segment: Rs. 3 crores (cumulative with cash segment requirements)
- Trading members in the currency derivatives segment: Rs. 1 crore (additional)
- Algorithmic trading members: Rs. 5 crores (minimum)

Net worth is computed as paid-up capital plus free reserves minus intangible assets minus non-allowable assets minus deferred revenue expenditure.

## 3. Obligations of Stock Brokers Towards Clients

### 3.1 Know Your Client (KYC) Requirements

Stock brokers are obligated to carry out thorough KYC of every client before opening a trading account. The three main obligations of a stock broker towards clients are as follows:

**First Obligation — Client Onboarding and KYC:** Stock brokers must collect, verify, and maintain KYC documents for every client. This includes identity proof, address proof, PAN card verification, income declaration, and trading experience details. Brokers must upload client KYC data to KYC Registration Agencies (KRAs) within the prescribed timelines. Failure to complete KYC before allowing trading constitutes a serious violation and may result in suspension of trading privileges for that client.

**Second Obligation — Risk Disclosure and Suitability Assessment:** Stock brokers must provide clients with a comprehensive risk disclosure document before opening accounts for trading in derivatives. In the case of retail investors, brokers are required to assess whether the client is suitable for derivatives trading based on income, net worth, and prior experience. Clients who do not meet minimum eligibility thresholds (annual income of Rs. 5 lakhs or minimum net worth of Rs. 10 lakhs for equity derivatives as per SEBI circular SEBI/HO/MRD-1/DOP/P/CIR/2024/23) must not be permitted to trade in futures and options. The risk disclosure document must be signed by the client, and the broker must retain a copy for at least five years.

**Third Obligation — Safeguarding Client Funds and Securities:** Stock brokers must ensure strict segregation of client funds from their own proprietary funds at all times. Client funds received from clients must be deposited only in designated client bank accounts, and brokers must not use client funds for their own purposes, for meeting obligations of other clients, or for any other purpose whatsoever. Similarly, client securities must be held only in separate client demat accounts or designated pool accounts. Brokers must transfer client securities to their own accounts only for the purpose of giving delivery against trades executed on behalf of clients. Running accounts must be settled at least once in 30 days (or 90 days based on client authorisation), and any excess client funds must be returned promptly.

### 3.2 Contract Note Issuance

Every stock broker must issue contract notes to clients for all trades executed within 24 hours of execution. The contract note must be digitally signed and contain the following information:

- Trade date, trade time, order number, and trade number
- Name and quantity of security bought or sold
- Price at which the trade was executed
- Brokerage charged (separately disclosed)
- All taxes and levies including STT, GST, stamp duty, exchange transaction charges, SEBI turnover fees, and IPFT charges
- Settlement number

Contract notes must be issued in the prescribed format. Failure to issue contract notes or issuance of incorrect contract notes is a serious violation.

### 3.3 Margin Collection and Reporting

Stock brokers are required to collect upfront margins from clients before placing orders. This is a critical client protection mechanism introduced through the Peak Margin framework (described in a separate circular). Key requirements include:

- Upfront collection of SPAN margin plus exposure margin for derivatives trades
- Collection of Value at Risk (VaR) margin plus Extreme Loss Margin (ELM) for equity delivery trades
- No use of one client's margin to fund another client's margin shortfall
- Daily reporting of client-wise margin collection to exchanges and clearing corporations

### 3.4 Reporting and Disclosure Obligations

Stock brokers must:

- Submit monthly compliance reports to the exchanges
- File quarterly Net Worth certificates with SEBI within 60 days of quarter end
- Disclose all related party transactions to exchanges on a quarterly basis
- Report suspicious transactions under the Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA), 2002 to the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU-IND)
- Report cyber security incidents to SEBI and relevant stock exchanges within 6 hours of detection

## 4. Client Fund Segregation and Settlement Obligations

### 4.1 Designated Client Bank Accounts

All client funds received must be held in designated client bank accounts maintained separately from the broker's own funds. Brokers must maintain a clear audit trail of all client fund movements. The broker must ensure that:

- Client fund accounts are maintained with Scheduled Commercial Banks or with clearing banks empanelled by exchanges.
- Client fund accounts must be clearly labelled as client funds accounts.
- No overdraft is permitted in client fund accounts.
- All client credits and debits must correspond to actual trade settlements or client authorised transactions.

### 4.2 Running Account Settlement

Running account of client funds refers to the arrangement where the broker retains client funds beyond the pay-out date instead of returning them immediately after each settlement. SEBI has permitted running accounts subject to the following strict conditions:

- The client must provide explicit written authorisation for running account settlement.
- The broker must settle the running account at least once in every 30 calendar days (for clients who have authorised 30-day settlement) or at least once in every 90 calendar days (for clients who have authorised 90-day settlement).
- The settlement is mandatory even if the client has open positions, subject to retaining adequate margin for open positions.
- An account statement must be sent to the client at the time of each settlement.
- Clients may revoke their running account authorisation at any time by giving notice to the broker.

### 4.3 Prohibition on Improper Utilisation of Client Securities

Brokers are strictly prohibited from:

- Using client securities for pledging, hypothecation, or any other purposes without explicit client consent.
- Transferring client securities to any account other than the designated pool account for delivery.
- Settling obligations of one client using securities of another client.
- Using client margins collected in excess of requirements for proprietary trading.

## 5. Grievance Redressal Mechanism

### 5.1 Internal Grievance Redressal

Every stock broker must have a designated Investor Grievance Redressal Officer (IGRO) responsible for resolving client complaints. The broker must:

- Provide a dedicated email address and phone number for grievance registration.
- Acknowledge complaints within 24 hours of receipt.
- Resolve complaints within 30 days of receipt or provide a detailed interim response.
- Maintain a complaint register in the prescribed format.

### 5.2 SEBI SCORES

Clients who are not satisfied with the broker's resolution may escalate complaints to SEBI through the SEBI Complaints Redress System (SCORES). SEBI will forward the complaint to the broker, who must respond with an Action Taken Report (ATR) within 21 days. The broker's response must be evidence-backed and must address each point raised in the complaint.

---

## 6. Penalties for Non-Compliance

Non-compliance with any provision of this Master Circular may attract the following actions:

- Warning letters
- Monetary penalties under Section 15HB of the SEBI Act, 1992 (up to Rs. 1 crore per violation)
- Suspension of certificate of registration
- Cancellation of certificate of registration
- Prosecution under relevant provisions of the SEBI Act, 1992

---

## 7. Applicability and Effective Date

This Master Circular is effective immediately from the date of issuance. All stock brokers, stock exchanges, and clearing corporations are advised to bring the contents of this circular to the notice of their members, constituents, and staff. This circular is issued in exercise of the powers conferred under Section 11(1) of the Securities and Exchange Board of India Act, 1992.
